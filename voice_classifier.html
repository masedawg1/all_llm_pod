Speaker Identification Architecture

Speaker Classification Policy Evolution

V1 — Host-Only Classifier

The first iteration of a multi-speaker classifier was created using four binary classifiers to extract about 9,000 audio files for the hosts from 98 podcast episodes. WhisperX handled ASR processing, and Pyannote embeddings drove speaker mapping. These steps produced the first 187 transcripts for the app.

Noteworthy: Guests were deliberately excluded. Guest audio was difficult to reliably gather at scale. Despite this, the system did not misclassify guests as hosts — a fifth "unknown" class was unexpectedly created during training, and low-confidence guest segments were correctly absorbed into it. Even so, the system was basic and fragile, and required a redesign.

Architecture lacked production robustness.

V2 — Guest-Aware Scaling

V2 was made possible via a custom HITL tool I created called Clownfish. Clownfish ingested the original V1 transcripts and let me manually review and re-map pyannote speaker_ids to structured custom_speaker labels, with side-by-side audio examples and video screengrabs for cross-validation.

Once a speaker was validated and assigned, the transcript was automatically backfilled with the corrected labels. Audio for each segment was then extracted into clean, per-speaker directories. This produced the first reliable, guest-inclusive dataset and established a disciplined labeling pipeline.

V2 — Core Signals
V2 Summary: • Integrated HITL pipeline (Clownfish) with clusterID/audio/video cross-validation • Massive scale-up: **85,619 total audio samples** (70/15/15 split) • Transcript backfill applied after each speaker confirmation Dataset Distribution (Pre-balancing): • Chamath: 22,930 (26.8%) | JCal: 21,915 (25.6%) • Sacks: 13,696 (16.0%) | Friedberg: 13,103 (15.3%) • Guest: 13,975 (16.3%) → Introduced robust "Guest" class for negative sampling Performance Metrics: • **Test Accuracy: 0.9798 (97.98%)** • Sacks F1: 0.9851 | Chamath F1: 0.9823 • Guest F1: 0.9879 (Highest recall at 0.9895) • JCal F1: 0.9708 (Main source of noise: 48 misclassified as Chamath) Key Shift: V2 moved from simple MLP feasibility to a higher-quality, pre-production pipeline. While training hit 100% accuracy, the test set's 98% performance across 12,843 samples proved the model's ability to generalize once guest segments and class-weight balancing were properly implemented, ensuring evaluation metrics were no longer inflated by host-only domain constraints.

V3 — Embedding Upgrade (NeMo / TitaNet)

Episode count grew to 387. V3 replaced Pyannote embeddings with Nvidia NeMo / TitaNet, immediately stabilizing the embedding space. Clusters became tighter, cosine distances became reliable, and label consistency improved across the entire dataset. Accuracy dipped slightly, but the geometry and confidence improved.

V4 — Duration Constraint & Production Reality

Whitepapers warn that sub-5s audio destabilizes embeddings, and this matched what I observed in my corpus.

Community advice was to drop short segments entirely — which makes sense: shorter audio is more likely to be mislabeled, and contains less acoustic identity. But roughly 50% of All-In segments are under 4 seconds, and discarding half the dataset this early wasn't acceptable.

My working hypothesis became:
1. Train the classifier only on host and guest audio longer than 5 seconds
2. Limit the classifier to segments longer than 5s, leaving all short segments untouched
3. Propagate speaker labels to short segments using NeMo cluster_id, which had proven remarkably reliable reliable across dozens of episodes

Classifier Removal — Structural Failure

Classifiers were ultimately dropped when spot-checks of V4 transcripts exposed high-confidence contradictions that defied the system's own logic. Seeing longer segments labeled with nearly 100% confidence while simultaneously being flagged as is_back_propagated: true—a state that should be physically impossible—was the turning point.

Worse still, those high-confidence, "back-propagated" segments that the model insisted were Friedberg were, in fact, Brad Gerstner. In retrospect, it’s wholly possible that buggy code produced the mislabeling and would have been worthy of troubleshooting. But at the time, I was so unsettled by the outcome—knowing such a glaring error could pass at scale with a perfect confidence score—that I decided to drop the classifier altogether.

Ultimately, I chose to strip the pipeline down. An imperfect label space sitting on top of overlapping embeddings. I'd rather have a simplified, transparent, HITL pipeline that has some tedious work than a prompt-and-pray black box that's confidently wrong.

Brad's embedding geometry explained the second failure: his cosine distances placed him directly on top of Sacks and Friedberg in embedding space, making host/guest separation impossible for the classifier. It wasn't confused — it was incapable of being correct.

It's unclear why custom_speaker: "brad" was never created. If the labeling had been incorrectly limited to hosts, I'd expect to see his segments attributed to JCal (who wasn't present in the episode) — but that didn't happen. That bolsters the case that Brad's voice was simply too phonemically close to the hosts for the classifier to work effectively, regardless of label space.

That's why the classifier layer was removed. A forced, incomplete label space sitting on top of overlapping embeddings isn't truth — it's noise with confidence scores.
