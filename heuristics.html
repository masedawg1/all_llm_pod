False Boundary Detection

Heuristics & Lexical Indicators

WhisperX establishes canonical segment and word-level timestamps, and NeMo is constrained to operate within those boundaries to establish speaker identity. Wrong attributions leave textual fingerprints. These heuristics confidently detect them.

Boundary Integrity Flags

1. turn contamination: ellipsis boundary
The speaker hadn't finished their thought — the boundary is likely drawn mid-utterance.

3. hyphen terminator
The speaker was cut off mid-word — the boundary was drawn at an interruption point, not a natural pause.
A"And the reason that works is because the under-" B"Right, exactly."
4. lowercase continuation
ASR capitalizes new speaker turns — a lowercase opening strongly suggests this is a continuation misattributed to a different speaker.
A"So that's the whole point." B"and that's why it matters."
5. missing terminator
The segment ends mid-sentence — the speaker almost certainly kept talking and the boundary is arbitrary.
A"I think what we're seeing here is a fundamental shift in how" B"Right, totally agree."
6. ellipsis + lowercase continuation
Both signals fire together — the two fragments almost certainly belong to the same speaker.
A"The reason it works is..." B"because the model has already seen it."
7. ellipsis + speaker flip
An unfinished sentence immediately followed by a new speaker — either the first speaker's tail was clipped or the boundary is misplaced.
A"So what I'm saying is..." B"We should probably move on."
8. backchannel bleed
Single-word backchannels are too short to build a clean embedding — diarization routinely misassigns them to the wrong speaker.
A"That's the entire thesis of the argument." B"Yeah."
9. discourse marker injection
Discourse markers signal a speaker resuming — their presence at a flip often means a continuous utterance was split and misattributed.
A"That's a really good point." B"So the way I think about it..."
10. period before conjunction
Conjunctions grammatically connect to what preceded them — a conjunction opening a new speaker turn suggests a single sentence was cut in half.
A"That's the end of the first argument." B"But there's another way to look at this."
11. third-person self-reference
The attributed speaker's own name appears in their segment — a strong signal the segment belongs to someone else entirely.
Friedberg"Less than the $1.4 billion in funding it has raised to Friedberg's been bringing this point up over and over..."
