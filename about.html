Project Background

How This Got Built

The goal was a persona-driven chatbot that could reproduce how people speak, not just what they say. The All-In Podcast was the obvious corpus — widely followed in tech circles, hundreds of episodes. The first challenge wasn't modeling language at all. YouTube transcripts lacked speaker attribution, so a custom pipeline was built to convert raw audio into speaker-labeled transcripts. That pipeline became the foundation of everything that followed.

V1 took a couple of months. ASR plus a voice classifier trained on host audio identified speakers; Mistral-7B ran the personas. On the surface it worked. Then a structural weakness surfaced: the classifier had only seen the four hosts, meaning guest speech could be silently forced into a host label. Looking back, that failure didn't appear often — an "unknown" bucket absorbed most guest audio — but the architecture was fragile, and I was embarrassed by the oversight, so this warranted a rebuild.

Days of focused work followed creating custom embeddings and classifiers to find and label speakers at scale. Clownfish made this possible. More on the subject of embeddings and classifiers here ↗

NeMo

Getting NVIDIA NeMo running locally was the turning point. It produced far more stable diarization than Pyannote. Streamlit tooling expanded around it — timeline analysis, facial recognition, cross-validation signals. Within a day or two it had grown too complicated to reason about. So it was stripped back.

What remained was Clownfish: NeMo produces stable speaker IDs, the interface groups clips by ID, you listen and map the ID to a name. Once mapped, the assignment propagates through the transcript. That simplicity let the pipeline scale from roughly 185 labeled episodes to more than 500 within a couple of months.

In parallel, speaker classification evolved alongside the transcript pipeline. A first classifier — 9,120 balanced clips, four hosts, Pyannote embeddings — hit 95.3% test accuracy with proper train/val/test splits for all speakers. A second generation scaled to 76,000+ host clips and 14,000+ guest clips across 114 guests. Two embedding tracks ran in parallel: Pyannote XGBoost reached 99.41% on binary host/guest detection; NeMo TitaNet XGBoost reached 99.38% on 4-host identification with a 3.96x cluster separation ratio — a fundamentally tighter geometry.

By every benchmark, strong results. But production behavior told a different story. Episode 136 made this concrete: Brad Gerstner was misclassified as David Friedberg. The embeddings revealed why — some of Brad's clips landed closer to host clusters than same-speaker clips do to each other. No classifier could reliably separate those cases. The embedding space itself overlapped.

That finding drove the final architectural decision: abandon classifier-based labeling, rely on NeMo's stable speaker_id backbone, and use HITL heuristic review to surface edge cases.
