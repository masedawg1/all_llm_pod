Project Background

How This Got Built

V1 · Mistral-7B V2 · NeMo V3 · Clownfish

The project started as a text style transfer helper — a service to rewrite content in another person's voice using the OpenAI API. Audio forensics pulled it in a different direction. It's an active area of AI research, and it scratched an old itch: building systems that generate structured artifacts and sharpening their quality as understanding of the problem deepens.

The All-In Podcast was the obvious corpus: widely followed in technology and venture circles, with a large back-catalog. YouTube provides transcripts, but they aren't usable. A custom transcription pipeline was built from scratch — audio in, structured speaker-labeled text out — and that became the training foundation for each host persona.

Build History

V1 mistral-7b · initial personas
▼
Status
Running within a couple of months. The personas were believable. Then a structural problem surfaced: the voice classifier had been trained exclusively on host audio, which meant guest speech could theoretically be forced into one of the host classes — contaminating training data at the source.
The failure wasn't observed in practice — an "unknown" class absorbed most guest speech — but the architecture was fragile, and I didn't trust it. Trust is load-bearing in a data pipeline.
V2 nvidia nemo · stable diarization
▼
Trigger
NeMo had been an earlier side quest — prior install attempts failed. Once working, it produced far more stable clustering and speaker segmentation than Pyannote. Streamlit tooling expanded quickly: timeline analysis, facial recognition, several other features. Then it contracted deliberately once the right scope became clear.
Outcome
What remained became Clownfish: a focused labeling service. NeMo generates speaker_id segments; audio grouped by that ID is reviewed by ear and mapped to a name. Once mapped, the assignment propagates through the entire transcript.
EXP voice classifier · 100k clips
▼
Failure mode
A stronger voice classifier was trained on more than 100,000 audio clips across the four hosts and over 100 guests. It still failed where it mattered most: short utterances, overlapping speech, laughter, backchannels.
Episode 136 made this concrete — Brad Gerstner was misclassified as David Friedberg.
Pivot
Rather than chasing classifier accuracy, the approach shifted: treat stable NeMo speaker_ids as the dataset backbone, and build heuristics to surface transcriptional artifacts and misclassifications that would otherwise pass silently into training data. Those heuristics were built directly into Streamlit, where they could be acted on immediately.
V3 clownfish · heuristic repair pipeline
▼
Result
The pipeline scaled from roughly 185 labeled episodes to more than 530 within a couple of months. Each episode more defensible than the last — not because the models got smarter, but because the process for catching their mistakes did.
